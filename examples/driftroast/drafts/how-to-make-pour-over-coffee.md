# How to make pour-over coffee

Pour-over coffee is a manual brewing method where you pour hot water over ground coffee in a filter, so gravity pulls the water through in a single pass. The control it gives you over grind, water, and pour is what makes it the clearest way to taste what a good bean actually does.

## What is pour-over coffee?

Pour-over is a filter method: coffee sits in a paper or metal filter above your cup, and you pour water over it by hand. Unlike a machine, you control every variable — grind, water temperature, and how fast you pour.

That control is the point. An espresso machine hides its variables behind a button; a pour-over exposes them, which is exactly why cafés use it to show off a single-origin bean. For example, the same Ethiopian coffee that tastes flat from a drip machine can turn floral and bright when you slow the pour and even out the extraction. The method rewards attention, and it punishes stale beans — which is why it is worth starting with coffee roasted recently rather than a bag that has sat for months.

## What you need

Four things: a dripper and filter, a scale, a kettle, and fresh coffee. A gooseneck kettle helps because it controls the pour, but any kettle works while you learn.

Freshness matters more than gear. Coffee off-gasses CO2 for days after roasting, and that gas is what creates the bloom later — so beans roasted last week behave very differently from beans roasted in spring. DriftRoast roasts to order and ships fresh for exactly this reason. Beans that reach you within 48 hours of roasting are far more forgiving to brew. Grind just before you brew, to a medium-fine texture roughly like table salt.

## How do you make pour-over coffee, step by step?

Weigh and grind at a 1:16 ratio — about 60 g of coffee per litre — then bloom, then pour in slow spirals until you hit your target weight. Start to finish is roughly three minutes.

Here is the full sequence:

1. Heat water to 195-205°F and grind your coffee medium-fine.
2. Put the filter in the dripper and rinse it with hot water; discard that water.
3. Add the grounds and shake level, then place the whole thing on your scale and zero it.
4. Bloom: pour about twice the coffee's weight in water, just enough to wet all the grounds, and wait 30-45 seconds.
5. Pour in slow, steady spirals from the centre outward, keeping the bed level, until you reach a 1:16 total.
6. Let it draw down; the whole brew should finish near the three-minute mark.

For example, for a single cup you would grind about 20 g, bloom with roughly 40 g of water, and pour to a 320 g total. Adjust from there by taste, not by rule.

## Why does the bloom matter?

The bloom releases trapped CO2 so water can actually reach the coffee. Skipping it leaves gas in the bed that pushes water away, and the result is a thin, uneven cup.

You can see it happen: pour that first splash of water and fresh grounds swell and bubble like a rising dough. That reaction is the freshness test built into the method — vigorous bloom means lively beans, while a flat, silent bloom usually means the coffee is old. Give it 30-45 seconds so the degassing finishes before your main pour, and the extraction that follows will be far more even.

## How do you fix a bad pour-over?

Diagnose by taste: sour and thin means under-extracted, harsh and bitter means over-extracted. Each points to a specific fix rather than a total do-over.

If the cup is sour, the water moved through too fast or too cool — grind a touch finer, or nudge the temperature toward 205°F. If it is bitter and drying, the opposite happened, so grind coarser or pour a little faster to shorten contact time. Change one variable at a time, because moving grind and temperature together tells you nothing about which one helped. For instance, a common beginner mistake is grinding too coarse and brewing too hot at once, then not knowing why the cup is both weak and harsh — separate the changes and the method becomes a set of small, legible adjustments instead of guesswork.
